<p dir="auto">Here is a mystery unsolved which was bugging me for <em>decades</em> - it's about the iconic white railway clocks that used to be on all railway stations at least in Germany. The thing with these clocks is, that once every minute, the hand for the seconds stops for a period of time which is noticeable <em>longer</em> than one second, and I always wondered why this is the case. My most favorite explanation (they do that so that they can fake that their trains are on time) doesn't hold after even the slightest of thinking it through.</p>
<p dir="auto"><a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_railway_clock" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_railway_clock" target="_blank">Turns out that these hands run faster by design, they wait on the top of every minute for a sync signal that synchronizes all clocks in the railway station before starting the new minute.</a></p>

<p dir="auto"> <span alt="2023-01-15-ios-keyboard > ^blip" src="2023-01-15-ios-keyboard#^blip" class="internal-embed markdown-embed inline-embed is-loaded"><div class="markdown-embed-title"></div><div class="markdown-preview-view markdown-rendered show-indentation-guide node-insert-event"><div data-callout-metadata="" data-callout-fold="" data-callout="summary" class="callout node-insert-event"><div class="callout-title" dir="auto"><div class="callout-icon"><svg width="16" height="16"></svg></div><div class="callout-title-inner"><strong>philipp</strong>:</div></div><div class="callout-content">
<p dir="auto">Here's a <a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://commoncog.com/case/iphone-keyboard/" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://commoncog.com/case/iphone-keyboard/" target="_blank">nice write up about how the iPhone keyboard was designed.</a> It's hard to imagine these days, but typing on touch screens back then was simply impossible. Touch screens were essentially merely more than left-click input devices, multi touch was unsupported, and the touch screens back then were <em>really</em> bad at handling hit zones close to one another.</p>
<p dir="auto">I am wondering what were the reasons to not explore <a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T9_(predictive_text)" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T9_(predictive_text)" target="_blank">T9 like input systems</a> more. T9 was well established back then, and you could achieve <em>insane</em> typing speeds with it. I'd guess that this would also hold true on touch screens since the buttons can be much bigger, and there are far less rows/columns to account for than on a <code>QWERTY</code> keyboard.</p>
<p dir="auto">The article mentions a similar approach and its main drawback - the fact that when you pause in the middle of a word, you have to memorize where you left off and are confronted with a word that might be very far off from what you'd intended to type. However, I have the feeling that this limitation alone could have been overcome with software alone.</p>

<p dir="auto"> <span alt="2023-01-07-not-the-customer > ^blip" src="2023-01-07-not-the-customer#^blip" class="internal-embed markdown-embed inline-embed is-loaded"><div class="markdown-embed-title"></div><div class="markdown-preview-view markdown-rendered show-indentation-guide node-insert-event"><div data-callout-metadata="" data-callout-fold="" data-callout="summary" class="callout node-insert-event"><div class="callout-title" dir="auto"><div class="callout-icon"><svg width="16" height="16"></svg></div><div class="callout-title-inner"><strong>philipp</strong>:</div></div><div class="callout-content">
<p dir="auto">It's an old saying that whenever you are not paying for it, you are the product. I don't think that this is true in most cases - instead, I think if you are not paying for it, someone else is the customer (which is a bit of a truism, but bear with me for a second). The classical example for people being the product would be Social Media, where people, or their interactions with ads, are the actual product the companies are selling.</p>
<p dir="auto">An example where people are neither the customer nor the product is B2B software, where the user interface is, sometimes hilariously, sometimes unbearably, horrible to use - <a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://blog.plover.com/prog/crap-warning-signs-2.html" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://blog.plover.com/prog/crap-warning-signs-2.html" target="_blank">SAP software being the canonical example</a>.</p>
<p dir="auto">The reason for that is that the goals of the person buying the software does not always align with the goals or wishes of the actual users - ease of use vs. price, for example. Concur, the tool discussed in the link above, is actually a good example where this tradeoff kind of makes sense: In many companies, the tool is crucial to have, but rarely used, and a shoddy UX would be worth the cost savings.</p>
<p dir="auto">Often, though, this phenomenon is due to misaligned goals, because <em>of course</em> does the UX have an effect on the efficiency of the people using it, especially when they rely on the tool for their day to day work. If cost saving is more important than mid term efficiency, then a poor UX is acceptable.</p>
<p dir="auto">As a side note, I have done my fair share of last-minute preparation of half-assed features just to get the software through a sales pitch, and I, too, have heard the lie of "fixing it later" once the contract is finalized. Typically, the presentation in the sales pitch is tailored around the capabilities of the software, and the minute someone asks the presenter to click somewhere outside of their script, things start to fall apart. Consequently, handwaving was a crucial skill for our sales colleagues.</p>

<p dir="auto"> <span alt="2023-01-07-security-by-floccinaucinihilipilification > ^blip" src="2023-01-07-security-by-floccinaucinihilipilification#^blip" class="internal-embed markdown-embed inline-embed is-loaded"><div class="markdown-embed-title"></div><div class="markdown-preview-view markdown-rendered show-indentation-guide node-insert-event"><div data-callout-metadata="" data-callout-fold="" data-callout="summary" class="callout node-insert-event"><div class="callout-title" dir="auto"><div class="callout-icon"><svg width="16" height="16"></svg></div><div class="callout-title-inner"><strong>philipp</strong>:</div></div><div class="callout-content">
<p dir="auto">I once worked with a company that was developing call center software: The software on the client PCs that shows information about the calling customer. Quickly I discovered that the protocol between the client and the server was not encrypted at all, passwords were transmitted in clear text and there was no server-side validation to access any customer records: Essentially, the server did whatever the client ask for, and inversely, all the server could do was to ask the client nicely.</p>
<p dir="auto">For example, if the server wanted to shut down a client remotely (for instance because the shift of the person on that computer has ended and they should no longer receive any data), all it did was sending what we dubbed a <code>would-you-kindly</code> request to the client to shut down, which the client could potentially ignore without any consequences at all. The session would stay active if the client dropped the request.</p>
<p dir="auto">Obviously, I raised this to both the lead architect and the lead product manager, and, equally obviously, they both already knew and told me that call center employees were too dumb to exploit this, because if they weren't, they wouldn't be working in a call center.</p>
<p dir="auto">I am not sure if there is a better word for it, but I'd like to call this approach "Security by <a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/floccinaucinihilipilification" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/floccinaucinihilipilification" target="_blank">floccinaucinihilipilification</a>".</p>

<p dir="auto"> <span alt="2023-01-05-301-redirects > ^blip" src="2023-01-05-301-redirects#^blip" class="internal-embed markdown-embed inline-embed is-loaded"><div class="markdown-embed-title"></div><div class="markdown-preview-view markdown-rendered show-indentation-guide node-insert-event"><div data-callout-metadata="" data-callout-fold="" data-callout="summary" class="callout node-insert-event"><div class="callout-title" dir="auto"><div class="callout-icon"><svg width="16" height="16"></svg></div><div class="callout-title-inner"><strong>philipp</strong>:</div></div><div class="callout-content">
<p dir="auto">While moving my blog to its new domain, I was setting up <code>URL</code> records from my old domain to the new one. Under the hood, <code>URL</code> records are a convenience service by my hosting service: they create an <code>A</code> record to their own redirection server, which then redirects the users to the contents of the <code>URL</code> record.</p>
<p dir="auto">Weirdly, it was not working. Now, I am used to weird intermediate states when it comes to DNS records, so I patiently waited, but this one was new: <code>dig</code> showed me the correct records, so did every DNS tool I could find, but still, the redirect did not work as expected, even after I flushed my browser's DNS cache.</p>
<p dir="auto">Turns out that the browser caches more than just DNS: They also cache <code>301</code> redirects, which is exactly what the redirection server returned. So my browser was following the old redirect and never even hitting the redirection server, oblivious to the fact that the redirection URL had changed somewhere in between.</p>

<p dir="auto"> <span alt="2023-01-05-echo-volume-fix > ^blip" src="2023-01-05-echo-volume-fix#^blip" class="internal-embed markdown-embed inline-embed is-loaded"><div class="markdown-embed-title"></div><div class="markdown-preview-view markdown-rendered show-indentation-guide node-insert-event"><div data-callout-metadata="" data-callout-fold="" data-callout="summary" class="callout node-insert-event"><div class="callout-title" dir="auto"><div class="callout-icon"><svg width="16" height="16"></svg></div><div class="callout-title-inner"><strong>philipp</strong>:</div></div><div class="callout-content">
<p dir="auto">Here is the fix to an annoying issue I recently had.</p>
<p dir="auto"><em>The problem:</em></p>
<p dir="auto">Alexa's volume is either too low or too loud relative to the media/music playback. So when you hear music at a regular volume and then ask Alexa something, either its feedback blasts holes in the nearest wall, or it is so low that you can barely hear it.</p>
<p dir="auto"><em>The solution:</em></p>
<p dir="auto">I only tested this with the Spotify skill, but I assume that this also works with other skills where you <em>remotely change the Echo's volume</em> , e.g. without directly interacting with the Echo. Here is what happens: Suppose the Echo is set up correctly at Volume 3, Alexa's volume is just fine. Then you start playback via Spotify. You increase the volume using the Spotify app: the Echo changes its volume. However, <em>Alexa's volume does not change with it</em>, so if you set the volume to e.g. 6 while hearing Spotify, Alexa's volume would still be at 3. If you now change the volume on the echo directly, or asking Alexa to change the volume, you change both the Echo volume and Alexa's volume at the same time - no matter if you're still using Spotify or not. So if, for example, after the above change you change your volume from 6 to 5 on the Echo, you also change Alexa's volume from 3 to 2. And if you turn the echo's volume all the way back to 3, Alexa's volume would be on the lowest setting.</p>
<p dir="auto">This also works the other way around.</p>
<p dir="auto"><em>The Fix</em>:</p>
<ul>
<li dir="auto">You need to use the Spotify skill (or, as mentioned above, any other skill that allows remotely changing the volume).</li>
<li dir="auto">Start playback.</li>
<li dir="auto">Using the Echo controls or voice commands, change the Echo's volume so that Alexa's voice responses are at the right level for you.</li>
<li dir="auto">Using Spotify's controls, change the volume to a level that matches Alexa's voice volume level.</li>
<li dir="auto">Both volumes should now be in synch and change simultaneously using the device's controls or voice commands.</li>
</ul>

replacement concepts:</p>
<ul>
<li dir="auto">Links, which is essentially a list of interesting links. Instead of opening a<br>

MVP.<br>

the current feature set for these features are not very satisfying, considering<br>

<p dir="auto">Nice idea, needs more work.</p>

<p dir="auto"><a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://www.mountaingoatsoftware.com/blog/to-re-estimate-or-not-that-is-the-question" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://www.mountaingoatsoftware.com/blog/to-re-estimate-or-not-that-is-the-question" target="_blank">Should you re-estimate your work while you do it or<br>
not?</a>,<br>
discussed in the context of agile estimation techniques. My stance is to never<br>
re-estimate after work has been started, even if it's a huge outlier. Because we<br>
tend to only re-estimate stuff where we have been too optimistic, and not items<br>
where we have been too pessimistic - which would then skew the final result.<br>
Also, even exceptional outliers are part of the usual business - they happen,<br>
and if we take them out by re estimating them, we assume that they will not<br>
happen in the future.</p>

<blockquote dir="auto">
<p>Italians are some of the fastest speakers on the planet, chattering at up to nine syllables per second. Many Germans, on the other hand, are slow enunciators, delivering five to six syllables in the same amount of time. <blockquote dir="auto">
<p>But the “why” is another question entirely. Pellegrino and his colleagues suspect that the answer has everything to do with the limits imposed by [...] how much information our brains can take in—or produce—at any one time. [...]<br>
De Boer agrees that our brains are the bottleneck. But, he says, instead of being limited by how quickly we can process information by listening, we’re likely limited by how quickly we can gather our thoughts. That’s because, he says, the average person can listen to audio recordings sped up to about 120%—and still have no problems with comprehension. “It really seems that the bottleneck is in putting the ideas together.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p dir="auto"><a href="#Misc" class="tag" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"><span class="" style="display: none;"></span>#Misc</a> <a href="#Technical" class="tag" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow"><span class="" style="display: none;"></span>#Technical</a> Debt</p>
<p dir="auto"><a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://engineering.squarespace.com/blog/2019/three-kinds-of-good-tech-debt" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://engineering.squarespace.com/blog/2019/three-kinds-of-good-tech-debt" target="_blank">Squarespace on three kinds of Good Technical Debt</a>. There are two interesting parts in this article.</p>
<blockquote dir="auto">
<p>“Tech debt” is a dirty word in the software engineering world. It’s often said with an air of regret; a past mistake that will eventually need to be atoned for with refactoring.</p>
<p>Financial debt isn’t universally reviled in the same way. Your friend takes out a mortgage to buy a house and what do you say? Congratulations!</p>
</blockquote>
<p dir="auto">The debt metaphor should be taken literally. Taking a debt means taking money someone with the promise of paying back in the future, then using that money for your benefit (possibly generating even more money) and paying it back with benefits. It's a win-win situation - in theory, that is.</p>
<p dir="auto">The other good point is this:</p>
<blockquote dir="auto">
<p>The key is to be intentional about what you invest time in and aware of the costs you’re taking on. [...] Good tech debt has clear, well-known limitations. Document these in code comments, READMEs, FAQs, and conversations with the people who’d care.</p>
<p>Used carefully, good tech debt will help you build software faster by focusing your time on the things that matter most.</p>
</blockquote>
<p dir="auto">At the end of the day, it boils down to "Take a debt consciouisly, aware of the risks and with the intention of paying it back".</p>

<p dir="auto"><a data-tooltip-position="top" aria-label="https://www.gwern.net/docs/math/1982-hofstadter.pdf" rel="noopener nofollow" class="external-link" href="https://www.gwern.net/docs/math/1982-hofstadter.pdf" target="_blank">Douglas Hofstadter on "Number Numbness" (1982)</a>. He is the author of "Goedel, Escher, Bach", and also coined "Hofstadter's law":</p>
<blockquote dir="auto">
<p>Hofstadter's Law: It always takes longer than you expect, even when you take into account Hofstadter's Law.</p>
</blockquote>
<p dir="auto">The article makes a great point of people not being able to grasp very large numbers:</p>
<blockquote dir="auto">
<p>I once taught a small beginning physics class on the thirteenth floor of Hunter College in New York City. From the window we had a magnificent view of the skyscrapers of midtown Manhattan. In one of the opening sessions, I wanted to teach my students about estimates and significant figures, so I asked them to estimate the height of the Empire State Building. In a class of ten students, not one came within a factor of two of the correct answer (1,472 feet with the television antenna, 1,250 without). Most of the estimates were between 300 and 500 feet. One person thought 50 feet was right-a truly amazing underestimate; another thought it was a mile.</p>
</blockquote>
<p dir="auto">Though the rest of the article focusses more on truly large numbers, like the difference between a million, a billion and a trillion, this quote shows that people have already difficulties with numbers which aren't <em>that</em> big.</p>
